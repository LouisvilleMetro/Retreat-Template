Getting to the finish Line...
Welcome to the Strategic Planning site for the 4th Annual Mayor’s Retreat. Here you will find the day’s agenda, prework and information to prepare you for the race ahead. Please review the pre-work materials prior to attending the event.

Harnessing the wisdom of the crowd.

Collectively, we will take time to evaluate our progress over the past year and review the impact of the Plan’s various goals and their initiatives on the community.

We will collaborate with our community partners
in order to problem solve around some of the challenges we currently face as they relate to reaching our finish line. In doing so, we will rely on the collective wisdom and skillsets of our partners.

Our Mission

Louisville Metro Government is the catalyst for creating a world-class city that provides its citizens with safe and vibrant neighborhoods, great jobs, a strong system of education and innovation, and a high quality of life.

Our Vision

Louisville will be a city of lifelong learning and great jobs, wellness and compassion.

Out Of The Gate

Speakers will provide 5-minute presentations on four of our key Community-Focused strategic initiatives. Presenters will focus on current state, proposed future projects, and areas for improvement. Participants will be given opportunities to share ideas on each of these strategic initiatives.

Front Runners

Utilizing 12 workgroups, and the collective wisdom of 120+ participants, both external and internal partners, will convene around 6 Metro Goals. We will co-create solutions to improve initiatives and key performance indicators. These goals are particularly significant, in that community support and engagement is essential for their success.

Winner’s Circle

Videos highlighting the successes achieved over the past 4 years will air throughout the Retreat. These will showcase Metro Goals and Strategic Initiatives that we have successfully completed during Mayor Fischer’s administration.

Meet Our Speakers!

Mayor Greg Fischer

Committed to growing jobs and creating a culture of innovation and entrepreneurship, Greg Fischer was elected Louisville's 50th mayor in 2010 -- and was sworn in for a second four-year term on January 5, 2015.Since taking office, Mayor Fischer has pursued three top goals: making Louisville a city of lifelong learning, a much healthier city and an even more compassionate community. These are the three pillars of his administration. As a businessman and entrepreneur, he brings a data-driven approach to city government with a goal of making it more efficient and accessible.Greg is a trustee for the U.S. Conference of Mayors and is past chair of the Conference's Metro Economies Committee which addresses economic problems facing the nation’s cities and their citizens. He was named a “Public Official of the Year” by Governing Magazine,the only U.S. mayor to earn that honor in 2013.

Ted Smith

Ted Smith was appointed by Mayor Greg Fischer as the Chief of Civic Innovation. Ted’s efforts include the 2012 IBM Smarter City Challenge-winning adoption of Asthmapolis sensor inhalers across Louisville to develop a new understanding of asthma. Ted is an Open Government/Open Data champion and is responsible for efforts to expand the entrepreneurial and innovation economy of Louisville. Ted also serves as Executive Director of a civic innovation laboratory – The Institute for Healthy Air, Water and Soil.   He holds a B.S. from Allegheny College and M.A. and Ph.D. in Cognitive Science from Miami University (OH) and a post-doc at MIT. He was named 2013 Government Technology’s “Top 25 Doers, Dreamers and Drivers.”     

Anthony Smith

Anthony Smith earned a bachelor’s degree from Northern Kentucky University. He served as the Director of Network Organizing for the Network Center for Community Change (NC3) in Louisville from 2008-13.  At NC3 he brought churches, businesses, civic groups and citizens together to address a wide variety of community challenges, from education improvement to vacant properties. He began his career in 1997 as a caseworker in the Jefferson County Attorney’s Office, preparing paternity cases for court.  He later worked for the Louisville Urban League and for Jefferson County Public Schools as a career planner in adult education. Smith is deeply involved in the community, including serving on the Board of Health, Habitat for Humanity, and 15,000 Degrees, part of the 55,000 Degrees initiative.

Stephen Reily

Stephen Reily is an attorney, entrepreneur, and civic leader whose philanthropic energy has focused in recent years on building the local food economy in Louisville and a more sustainable future for Kentucky’s farmers. In 2011 he co-founded Seed Capital Kentucky, a non-profit whose projects have included research, grants to farmers, and developing (in partnership with Metro Louisville and Mayor Greg Fischer) the 24-acre West Louisville FoodPort. He is Chairman of the Greater Louisville Project, an independent, non-partisan civic initiative that acts as a catalyst for action by presenting research, data and analytic tools supporting the agenda for long-term progress in Metro Louisville. Stephen is the Chairman & Managing Director of IMC, a marketing services agency that develops new products for some of the world’s largest consumer brands, and Vibrant Nation, IMC’s digital marketing division and the leading online community and influencer network for women 45+.

Theresa Zawacki

Theresa Zawacki has been with the Louisville Metro Department of Economic Department since September 2011. Ms. Zawacki is the city’s Food Policy Advisor, and coordinates Louisville’s Brownfields Program. Previously, Ms. Zawacki was an Assistant County Attorney with the Jefferson County Attorney’s Office where she represented the Louisville Metro Planning Commission, the Louisville Metro Board of Zoning Adjustment, the Louisville Metro Landmarks Commission, and the Louisville/Jefferson County Environmental Trust, as well as all Louisville Metro staff involved with land use, planning and zoning. Ms. Zawacki began her legal career as an associate at the law firm of Greenebaum, Doll & McDonald (now Greenebaum Bingham Doll), where she practiced in both the land use and environmental practice groups.   Ms. Zawacki received her BA from Transylvania University in 1998, and both her JD and Masters of Community Planning from the University of Cincinnati in 2003.

Key Community Focused
Strategic Initiative Summaries

Globalization

Over the past decade, New Americans have played a significant role in Louisville’s overall growth, accounting for nearly half
of the metro’s population increase from 2000 to 2012.

Entrepreneurship

Entrepreneurship in popular terms refers to the creation of high-growth firms. Young, fast-growing companies create a substantial positive impact on Louisville’s economy by leveraging local talent, skill and capital often creating the leading edge of industries in our economy.

FoodPort

The West Louisville FoodPort is poised to become one of the most transformative urban reinvestment projects of the decade. Located on a 24-acre campus at 30th and W. Market Streets, the former site of the National Tobacco Works warehouse, the FoodPort will locate food-related businesses in one place where they can buy food from local farmers and hire neighbors for available jobs. Video

One Love Louisville

Over a 5 year period (2009-2013), an average of 56 Louisville residents died as a result of homicide, 86 died as a result of suicide and 106 died as a result of drug overdose. The Office for Safe and Healthy Neighborhoods (OSHN) was created to reduce these averages over time and is now in its implementation phase called One Love Louisville: Be the One to Make a Difference. For additional information please feel free to review the Blueprint for Safe & Healthy Neighborhoods.

Front Runners (Case Studies)

Please review the Case Study materials for your assigned Metro Goal. Your advanced preparation will enable us to quickly take the lead in co-creating solutions to improving Goals, Initiatives and Key Performance Indicators.

Metro Goal 4 - Case Study

Increase number of online services offered by Metro Government by 15% annually between FY13 and FY19 and increase the Open Data, Open Metadata and Open Content about city assets and services available online. For additional information please feel free to review the DoIT Strategic Roadmap.

Metro Goal 13 - Case Study

Improve the health of Louisville Metro residents by developing initiatives to support the community’s health improvement plan - Healthy Louisville 2020 (HL2020). For additional information please feel free to review the Healthy Louisville 2020 Plan.

Metro Goal 15 - Case Study

Capitalize on our diverse population by increasing representation within Louisville Metro workforce, on Mayoral appointed boards and commissions, and through procurement opportunities.

Metro Goal 19 - Case Study

Establish, encourage, and celebrate creative environments, events, and partnerships that yield local innovations, learning opportunities and successful ventures and make Louisville a destination for curious, creative individuals.

Metro Goal 20 - Case Study

Implement Sustain Louisville by protecting the environment and reducing our carbon footprint; increasing green infrastructure and tree canopy; ensuring the health, wellness
and prosperity of all citizens; and embedding sustainability into the culture of the Louisville community. For additional information please feel free to review the Sustain Louisville Plan.

Metro Goal 21 - Case Study

In 2012, Louisville Metro Government gathered with citizens and stakeholders to create a 21-goal, 6-year strategic plan. Metro Goal #21 was, at this time, titled “Promote Volunteerism and Giving”. It focused on the Mayor’s Give-A-Day program, the Partnership for Compassionate Cities, and volunteerism with Metro agencies and by Metro employees.
